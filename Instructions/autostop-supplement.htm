One of our users, Laz, sent in his experience with installing the autostop chip. It is printed here, as it may provide some pointers or additional tips during installation.

Start with directions downloaded from Pachislo USA, Inc.

What you need:

Three to four conductor, 18-22 gauge stranded wire

Package of small cable or zip ties about 1/8 wide by 3” long

Wirenuts, or butt connectors, or wire taps

Rosin core 60/40 .032 diameter solder

Multimeter (maybe)

 

 

 

Besides the thin wirewrap wire, you may want to purchase some three to four conductor, 18-22 gauge stranded wire. It works better then wirewrap for running to the door of machine. It is stiffer and color-coded so you can tell where each wire goes. (You can see pictures below.) This type of wire can be ordered locally, such as from a Radio Shack, or you can order it over the internet. You will not want it any larger or it may be too large to solder to the chip. You can also use a 4 conductor 'bundled' wire. If you are running the wires individually, you will  probably want to bundle them together with zip ties or some other method. There is a product called split loom tubing that allows entry and exit of the wires at locations of your choice.  

 

There are different ways you can connect the wires from the chip to the wires at the coin mechanism::

 

Simple wire nuts, electrical tape.

 This requires you to cut the wire at the coin mech, and splice in the wire from the modchip. Advantage: They are fast and can be removed easily. Disadvantage: They are not the safest or the most professional looking.

 

Crimp on butt splice connectors, regular and heat shrinkable type

 Once again, this requires that you cut the wire at the coin mech, and splice the wire from the modchip in. Advantage: Quick and look better; more permanent. Disadvantage: Permanent. If you need to make a change, you have to cut the old butt splice off, and discard it. The small size that will be needed does not always allow for more than one wire per side if you use the regular type. The heat shrinkable type may have a larger opening at the ends allowing you to put multiple wires in to be crimped.

 

Wire tap connectors

 Very neat and professional looking. They are a little hard to find sometimes.

Advantage: You don't need to cut the wire at the coin mech. Disadvantage: Need to strip and crimp on male tab connector for the wire you are splicing in; cost.

 

If something does not work right with the modification, you *will* need a meter to troubleshoot the problem. Pachislo Usa will be happy to help.

 

 

 

Installation Notes:

Your board should look exactly like the one in the picture at Pachislo USA. If it looks even a little different, do not solder onto a part that looks different! You may find a part that is a different color or style, or the part may be in a different orientation. An example: An example; on the boards from my Babel and Azteca Returns machines- the C9 capacitor was not only vertical but of a different style which made it difficult for me to solder onto. It is important to solder the wires onto the correct side of the part or the modification not work. I know, I did it wrong and had to do it all over again. Let me tell you it is a lot easier to do it right the first time! I am new to doing stuff on electronics. I did not realize it mattered what side I soldered onto. I was thinking it was easier to solder on the wires from the outside edge of the board. [Editor clarification: If, in a picture, it looks like the wire is on the left side of a part, do not solder the wire onto the right side of the part!]

 

On the 2nd instruction page, I found that the wire lengths for pins 5,6,7,8 worked best if they are about 1 1/4 inch long. Start with a longer wire and strip insulation off one end. Using your teeth rather than wire cutters for the thin wire works well when you are stripping longer wires. [Editor note: Teeth! The company is not recommending this!] Strip off ¼ inch of insulation, and then use wirecutters or a razor to cut it down to 1/16 to 1/8 inch. After you have successfully stripped wire off one end, cut the wire to the desired length (1 1/4"). You now have to strip the other end. You can use your teeth again but the wire brakes real easy. I used a utility knife and very carefully cut off only as much insulation as needed. For pin 1, a wire length of 2 to 2 ¼ inches worked out well.

 

For pin 2, 3 and 4, I used the heavier 18-22 gauge wire. I used a soldering iron with a 15 to 30 watt setting. The 15-watt setting worked best. After wires are soldered onto chip, I used a hot glue gun to make the socket stay on the reelstop board. Since these are heavier wires, you will probably have to use a needlenose pliers to hold each wire near the socket while bending it. If you don't hold onto the wire at where it is soldered to chip you may break it off. It does not take much since the socket itself has very thin wire. You can see how I bent the wires in a picture below.

 

When running the wires to the game start lever and the coin mechanism, I followed other bundled wires that were already in the machine. This way, I could use cable clamps that were already part of the machine. At some places, I used cable ties to keep all the wires secure and not get in the way of moving parts. A hot glue gun also worked to secure wires when there was nothing to secure them to. Some machines will have a plastic coil that wraps around wires near the door. If your machine has this plastic coil, you can run your wires from the modchip through it.

 

 

When splicing the wires into the game start lever and the coin mechanism I tried lots of things. First, I tried scraping off some of the insulation from the coin mech wire and wrapping the wire from the modchip around it. Then, I realized I should probably solder the wires on and tape it all up. This didn't look so good.

 

So, I took that apart and tried again. I stripped 1/4 inch insulation off the end of the wire from the modchip and decided to use a crimp on butt connector. If you use a crimp on connector you need a real small one. Only problem with them was that the plastic end was not big enough to handle two wires so I used a torch and heated a pointed scratch all. Stick it in the plastic end and widened the end. Do not heat the plastic end, this will not work. If you can find them, use the heat shrink butt connecters. They are wider at the ends. I did not know of these when I did this modification. Another way to splice in a wire, and I did not think of this at the time, is a connector I have seen a lot in automotive wiring - a wiretap type connector. The ones I have seen have been blue or gray. You place the wires in it without cutting or removing the insulation and you squeeze the two halves together. They are sometimes a little big and you need to get the one for your size wire. [Editor note: We recommend wire taps]

 

My finished product looks more professional, at least to me. Works well and does what it is supposed to do. This sounds like a lot but after you do one reelstop board, the rest are easy. From start to finish the second machine took me maybe two to two and a half hours. This is un-connecting and taking out the reel assembly, disconnecting the reel stop board, soldering up the chip to the board, running the wires, splicing in the ends and putting it back together. It helped that I was already familiar with the machine and how it came apart. Most reel assemblies come out in one piece. To take out the reel stop board you can either unscrew the board and backing plate or unclip just the board from the backing plate. If you do this be careful with the underside of the board and if you plug it back in to test something on it you can’t touch any metal with it. If you leave it in the backing plate these two things are not a problem. [Editor note: you won't have to take out the reelsets on all machines to get to the reelstop board. This does seem to be an issue with Babel and Azteca Legend though]

 

This is meant to be a supplement to the directions that you can download from Pachislo USA, and from my personal experience the directions didn’t explain enough for someone like me who never worked on something like this before but wanted to learn and have fun fixing, modifying, and having fun with my pachislo machines. I hope this helps in making this modification easier and fun to-do.
